# The Relativistic Searchlight is the Future of Telescopes and Radar



There is a very interesting but often overlooked aspect of Einstein's relativity. It is called the Relativist Search light effect or Relativistic Aberration.



It is often overlooked because you only notice it when you, the observer, is moving at greater than 80% of the speed of light.



Most of our experiments and explanation of relativity describes a stationary observer looking at an object moving near the speed of light. Or they examine the time dilation of the moving perspective.



But there is a unique effect on light when you move near the speed of light. You see the direction you are going towards appear brighter. 



This is because as you are approaching the speed of light, you collect more photons from the area in front of you and less from the area behind you.



A beautiful visual demonstration of this is the game “A shorter speed of light”. In this game you move the same speed the whole time but you scale the speed of light down. So eventually your normal speed is 80% of the speed of light.



We can use this in telescopes and radar technology. Rather than accelerate the entire observer, we can create a quantum relativistic Searchlight using only part of the sensor. 



By accelerating particles in the sensor to near the speed of light, any data that they collect will be distorted by the relativistic searchlight effect. This has the potential of vastly improving the range of our telescopes and radar technology.
