### Short summary: biophotons, neural filaments, and brain simulation

A useful way to frame these papers is that they point toward a possible **missing physical layer** in brain simulation: not replacing electrochemical neuroscience, but adding faint light emission, intracellular structure, and mitochondrial coordination as potentially relevant mechanisms.

The first pillar is that living tissue emits **ultraweak photons**. Kobayashi, Kikuchi, and Okamura showed that the human body emits faint visible-range light with daily rhythmic variation, likely linked to energy metabolism; the emission is far below naked-eye sensitivity, but measurable with sensitive CCD imaging. This matters for brain simulation because the brain is extremely energy-intensive, and if photon emission tracks oxidative metabolism, it could act as a readout—or possibly a weak signaling side-channel—of cellular metabolic state (Kobayashi, Kikuchi, and Okamura).

The second pillar is the possibility of **optical structure inside neurons**. Kumar et al. modeled myelinated axons as possible photonic waveguides and raised the question of whether biophotons could carry signals in addition to known electrochemical signaling. Rahnama et al. went further theoretically, proposing that mitochondrial biophotons could interact with microtubules and influence membrane electrical activity, including quantum-mechanical interaction models between biophotons and cytoskeletal structures. For simulation, the conservative takeaway is not “neurons are quantum computers,” but that intracellular geometry and optical/electromagnetic effects may deserve more modeling attention than point-neuron or simple compartment models usually give them (Kumar et al.; Rahnama et al.).

The third pillar is **mitochondrial communication**. Mould et al. reported that physically and chemically separated mitochondria could alter each other’s oxygen-consumption behavior, suggesting a non-chemical communication mechanism, potentially electromagnetic or ultraweak-photon mediated. This is especially important for brain simulation because mitochondria are not merely “power supplies”; they regulate ATP availability, calcium dynamics, oxidative stress, apoptosis, and synaptic function. If mitochondria can coordinate state through non-chemical signaling, then realistic brain simulation may need to model energy metabolism as a distributed, communicative network rather than a passive scalar constraint (Mould et al.).

The big implication is that high-fidelity brain simulation may eventually require at least three coupled layers: **electrical spiking**, **biochemical/metabolic state**, and **subcellular electromagnetic/photonic dynamics**. Today, the strongest evidence supports ultraweak photon emission and mitochondrial metabolic relevance; the more speculative parts are whether those photons carry functional information in neurons, whether neurofilaments or microtubules conduct meaningful “quantum information,” and whether this produces anything like “mitochondrial light computing.” Those ideas are plausible enough to investigate, but not yet established enough to treat as standard neuroscience.

In practical terms, these papers suggest a research roadmap for brain simulation: first model mitochondria as dynamic agents affecting neural excitability; then test whether ultraweak photon emission correlates with neural firing, metabolic load, or network state; finally, experimentally determine whether intracellular structures can guide, modulate, or decode those photons at biologically meaningful signal-to-noise levels.
