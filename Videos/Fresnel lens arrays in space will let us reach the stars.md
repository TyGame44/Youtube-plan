# Fresnel lens arrays in space will let us reach the stars



Richard Feynman once said 



“To every man is given the key to the gates of heaven. The same key opens the gates of hell.



And so it is with science.”



This technology could use new advances in 3d-printed microlenses. Or we could use adaptive fresnel lenses. Both of these offer advances and simplify the final design. The 3d-printed microlenses could take advantage of rotation rather than lateral movement for targeting and focussing. This could be made flexible with a multi-layer adaptive design. This design would adjust the focus by rotating the lenses relative to each other. Rather than rotating the whole array, we can emulate it by rotating the microlenses. We could use simple magnetic rotation of the microlenses to control an entire array.



We can produce silicone fresnel lenses in space to power our exploration into the stars. We could also use this technology to shoot missiles out of the sky.



If we wanted to keep the technology safe for defense use, we could design the focus to have a maximum height above the ground. This would prevent the technology from being used to target civilians on the ground. 



It is up to us what we build with this technology.



I propose a multi-stage rollout.



We will start with moon based manufacturing and development. This will include a 3d printer for sintering the surface of the moon. The main goal of this stage is to enable rapid development of silicone lenses on the moon's surface.

During this time we can easily make a space elevator on the moon with existing material science. (Kevlar would work).



Now we have a moon colony, a space elevator, and a production system for silicone lenses



Now we build the martian 3d printer / the solar printer.



By taking advantage of the weather system on mars and the dust storms, we can sinter our entire martian colony layer by layer. We would print the design like an EUV lithography mask. Then let the dust add another layer then sinter that layer.



This lets us develop and further understand the technology.



Now onto asteroid mining. We can address some of our issues by focusing on the reverse of the rocket problem. If we wanted to send ultralight weight nano-bots or some sort of extremophile bacteria to the asteroids, it would be much easier.



We could even leverage existing starlink satellites and EMF accelerated payloads.



But we may be able to do this without any payload sent to the asteroids entirely.

We could use a lot of approaches. We could liquify and spin the entire asteroid to sort the materials like a centrifuge. We could then pulse certain parts of the asteroid to eject specific materials and manufacture logic and functionality onto the asteroid itself.



This sounds harder than nanobots but a combination of the two might be ideal.



If we are using nanobots, we can power tiny gears on them using polarized light.



At this point we can manufacture a device that creates solar sails on the asteroid and leverages a Dzhanibekov like effect to periodically flip the solar sails using minimal energy to modify its orbit at the apex of its orbital to guide the asteroid back to the earth or the moon.



Infinite gold…



But that's just a theory. An asteroid mining theory. And thanks for watching.
