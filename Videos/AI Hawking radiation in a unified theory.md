# Quantum-Horizon Expansion of Hawking Radiation
Core idea

Hawking radiation is traditionally understood as a quantum-field effect caused by curved spacetime near the event horizon of a black hole. In the standard model, the black hole’s gravitational field mixes quantum field modes, so that what appears to be vacuum near the horizon is observed as thermal radiation by a distant observer.

This proposal expands that picture by treating the black hole not only as a curved spacetime geometry, but as a macroscopic quantum object. In this view, the singularity, or whatever quantum-gravity structure replaces the classical singularity, is not merely a mathematical endpoint. It is the deepest quantum state of the black hole. The event horizon is then not just a passive causal boundary, but the externally visible boundary condition of that internal quantum object.

The hypothesis is:

Hawking radiation arises from quantum field interactions at a horizon whose effective surface is shaped by the black hole’s underlying quantum state. The usual curved-spacetime field-mode mixing occurs on, or near, a dynamically fluctuating quantum horizon.

Standard Hawking radiation

In the usual explanation, Hawking radiation does not come from particles escaping from inside the black hole. Once matter crosses the event horizon, it cannot send ordinary light or particles back out.

Instead, the radiation is produced by quantum fields near the horizon. The black hole’s curved spacetime changes the relationship between field modes near the horizon and field modes far away. As a result, a distant observer detects a thermal flux of particles.

The escaping radiation carries positive energy away. To conserve energy, the black hole effectively absorbs negative energy, causing its mass to decrease. Over immense timescales, this leads to black hole evaporation.

Proposed expansion

The expansion is to ask what physically underlies the curved-spacetime field-mode effect.

Rather than treating the horizon as a purely classical boundary, we treat it as a quantum surface or quantum interface. This surface is not necessarily a material shell, but an effective horizon-scale structure produced by the black hole’s quantum state.

The black hole’s internal quantum object — classically represented as the singularity — may define or influence the microscopic degrees of freedom associated with the horizon. These horizon degrees of freedom fluctuate, vibrate, or evolve quantum mechanically. The background quantum field then interacts with this fluctuating horizon structure.

In this interpretation, Hawking radiation is still caused by curved spacetime mixing quantum field modes, but the mode mixing occurs against a quantum-dynamic horizon rather than a purely classical one.

In simplified form:

Classical version:
Curved spacetime near the event horizon mixes quantum field modes, producing Hawking radiation.

Expanded version:
A black hole’s internal quantum state creates a fluctuating quantum horizon. Quantum fields interacting with this horizon undergo mode mixing, producing Hawking radiation.

Important causal constraint

The proposal should not say that the singularity sends ordinary signals outward to the event horizon. In standard relativity, anything inside the event horizon is causally trapped. Light, particles, or vibrations from the interior cannot propagate outward through the horizon.

A safer formulation is:

The singularity, or quantum-gravity core, is not directly transmitting signals outward. Rather, the black hole is one quantum-gravitational system whose internal state and horizon state are linked nonlocally or holographically. The horizon is the external expression of the black hole’s total quantum state.

This avoids violating the causal structure of the black hole while preserving the central intuition: the horizon may not be an empty mathematical boundary. It may be the observable quantum interface of the black hole.

Relation to the event horizon

In classical general relativity, the event horizon is a null surface, not a physical wall. Massive particles cross it in finite proper time. However, from the perspective of an outside observer, matter falling toward the horizon appears increasingly redshifted and time-dilated.

This proposal uses the outside-observer/horizon description as physically meaningful at the quantum level. The event horizon becomes an effective quantum boundary where information, energy, and field modes are transformed.

This resembles ideas such as the stretched horizon, membrane paradigm, holography, and black hole complementarity, but the emphasis here is different:

The surface responsible for Hawking radiation may be a quantum-vibrational horizon generated by the black hole’s deeper quantum structure.

What “vibration” means here

“Vibration” should not mean a normal mechanical shaking of a solid surface. A black hole does not have an ordinary material surface at the event horizon.

Instead, vibration can mean:

Quantum fluctuations of horizon degrees of freedom.

Time-dependent microscopic geometry near the horizon.

Oscillation of the black hole’s quantum state.

Dynamic boundary conditions imposed on quantum fields.

Thermal fluctuations associated with black hole entropy and temperature.

So the phrase “quantum vibrating event horizon” should be understood as an effective description of microscopic horizon dynamics, not as a literal surface wobbling in space.

How this produces Hawking radiation

The proposed mechanism is:

The black hole contains, or is, a quantum-gravitational object.
Its internal quantum state is represented externally by horizon-scale quantum degrees of freedom.
These degrees of freedom form an effective fluctuating quantum horizon.
Background quantum fields interact with this horizon.
The curved spacetime and fluctuating boundary conditions mix field modes.
Distant observers detect the result as Hawking radiation.
The radiation carries energy away, so the black hole loses mass and the horizon shrinks.

This keeps the standard Hawking mechanism but gives it a deeper physical substrate.
